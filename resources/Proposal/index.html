Goals

Research Goals

Goal 1: Investigate the relationship between choice of communication strategy in software development projects and negotiation of meaning .

How do complex problem solving conversations form consensus and converge?

Goal 2: Investigate the relationship between communication strategy and identity in software development communities of practice.  

How does the chosen communication strategy affect the identity by affecting the trajectory of the individual within the community and vice versa? For example, if a participant identifies as more central to the group, do they play arbiter more often? 

Goal 3:  Construct and evaluate classroom activities that expose SE students to strategic communication choices.

How can we build awareness of strategic communication among our students and give them the skills to think of communication as problem solving?

Speech Act Theory

speech is the combination of several types of acts, starting from the mere utterance, linguistic and semantic act.

  • Locutionary -  mere utterance, linguistic and semantic
    utterance of the questions and answers, sending of the email
  • Illocutionary - socially intended significance
    client asking questions OR
    making a suggestion as a social act of expressing or questioning for the benefit of the receiver
  • Perlocutionary - actual effect of the utterance
    Denise gets a chance to prove her knowledge,  
    Benjamin - encouraging discussion and showing that he is thinking about the bigger picture as an active and core member

Discourse Analysis

Set of analytical techniques based on communication analysis

study patterns in communication behavior - (Gee '99)

 

(discourse) studying communication for its linguistic aspects as well as

(Discourse) ways of participation and representation with its linguistic nuances

 

Process any discourse in terms of who, when, what surroundings, what precedes it, how it is intended to be perceived, what it portrays about a way of thinking or being

 

"socially situated identity" of both the creators and receivers of that discourse

identity affects discourse produced and for whom

Grounded Theory

Instead of starting with a specific hypothesis, we are employing a grounded theory approach, where we start with a generic question and analyze the data in different ways to arrive at the more specific research questions. We adopt the version of Grounded Theory that Strauss and Corbin 1990 describe, with three phases that can overlap.

 

In the first phase, open coding, that data is tagged using conceptual codes that are developed by studying the data initially.

In the second phase, axial coding, the relationship, especially causal, between the codes is explored.

In the third phase, selective coding, a subset of the codes and relationships are used to make a theory.

(Strauss and Corbin '90)
(Zieris and Prechelt '14).
